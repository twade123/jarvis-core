# Complete Chart Pattern Encyclopedia

> Chart patterns represent crowd psychology crystallized in price action. The validator identifies these on M15 charts and uses them to confirm or reject trade setups. Patterns are stronger on higher timeframes but M15 patterns within H1/H4 trends are the bread and butter.

## Pattern Categories
- **Reversal**: Trend ends, new direction begins (H&S, double top/bottom, wedges, rounding)
- **Continuation**: Trend pauses, then resumes (flags, pennants, triangles, rectangles)
- **Bilateral**: Can break either way (symmetrical triangle, rectangle)

---

## REVERSAL PATTERNS

### Head and Shoulders (Bearish Reversal)
- **Structure**: Left shoulder (swing high), head (higher high), right shoulder (lower high than head, roughly equal to left shoulder). Neckline connects the two troughs.
- **Entry**: Break below neckline on increased volume. Conservative: wait for retest of neckline from below.
- **Target**: Distance from head to neckline, projected down from neckline break
- **Stop**: Above right shoulder
- **Reliability**: HIGH — one of the most reliable reversal patterns
- **Failure**: If price breaks above head after neckline break = pattern failed, often leads to aggressive move up
- **Key rule**: Right shoulder should be lower than head. If right shoulder makes new high = NOT H&S.
- **Volume**: Should decline from left shoulder → head → right shoulder. Break should have volume spike.

### Inverse Head and Shoulders (Bullish Reversal)
- **Structure**: Mirror of H&S — left shoulder (swing low), head (lower low), right shoulder (higher low)
- **Entry**: Break above neckline
- **Target**: Head-to-neckline distance projected up
- **Stop**: Below right shoulder
- **Reliability**: HIGH
- **Note**: Neckline doesn't have to be perfectly horizontal — sloping necklines are common

### Double Top (Bearish Reversal)
- **Structure**: Two swing highs at approximately the same level, separated by a trough (neckline)
- **Entry**: Break below neckline (the trough between the two tops)
- **Target**: Height of pattern (top to neckline) projected down from break
- **Stop**: Above the double top level
- **Reliability**: HIGH — very common and reliable
- **Key rule**: Tops don't need to be exactly equal (within 1-3% is fine). Second top often slightly lower.
- **Confirmation**: RSI divergence between the two tops (second top lower RSI) strengthens the signal significantly

### Double Bottom (Bullish Reversal)
- **Structure**: Two swing lows at approximately the same level. Mirror of double top.
- **Entry**: Break above the peak between the two bottoms
- **Target**: Height of pattern projected up
- **Stop**: Below the double bottom level
- **Reliability**: HIGH
- **Confirmation**: RSI divergence (second bottom higher RSI) = very strong

### Triple Top / Triple Bottom
- **Structure**: Three tests of the same level (resistance for top, support for bottom)
- **Entry**: Break beyond the pattern after third test
- **Target**: Pattern height projected from break
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM-HIGH — less common but each failed test strengthens the eventual break
- **Key insight**: Three tests of a level without breaking = very strong eventual reversal when it finally breaks

### Rounding Top (Bearish Reversal)
- **Structure**: Gradual arc from uptrend → flat → downtrend. Looks like an inverted bowl.
- **Entry**: Break below the pattern's support (left side starting point)
- **Target**: Height of the rounding pattern
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM — slow-forming, good for swing trades, less common on M15
- **What it means**: Gradual shift from buyers to sellers. No sudden reversal — momentum slowly dies.

### Rounding Bottom (Bullish Reversal)
- **Structure**: Gradual arc from downtrend → flat → uptrend. Looks like a bowl/saucer.
- **Entry**: Break above resistance at pattern's rim
- **Target**: Depth of the rounding pattern
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM — same as rounding top, slow-forming

## CONTINUATION PATTERNS

### Bull Flag (Bullish Continuation)
- **Structure**: Sharp up-move (flagpole), followed by slight downward-sloping consolidation channel (the flag)
- **Entry**: Break above the flag's upper trendline
- **Target**: Flagpole height projected from breakout point
- **Stop**: Below the flag's low
- **Reliability**: HIGH — one of the best continuation patterns
- **Key rules**:
  - Flagpole should be strong and steep (3+ candles of momentum)
  - Flag should retrace 30-50% of the pole (not more than 61.8%)
  - Flag duration: 5-20 bars is ideal (M15: 1.25-5 hours)
  - Volume should decrease during flag, increase on breakout
- **Thesis alignment**: Flag formation during EMA fan expansion = very high probability

### Bear Flag (Bearish Continuation)
- **Structure**: Sharp down-move (flagpole), followed by slight upward-sloping consolidation (flag)
- **Entry**: Break below flag's lower trendline
- **Target**: Flagpole height projected down
- **Reliability**: HIGH — mirror of bull flag

### Pennant (Continuation)
- **Structure**: Like a flag but the consolidation is a small symmetrical triangle (converging trendlines) rather than a channel
- **Entry**: Break in the direction of the prior trend (pole direction)
- **Target**: Pole height from breakout
- **Stop**: Beyond the opposite side of the pennant
- **Reliability**: HIGH — slightly more explosive than flags because of compression
- **Key difference from flag**: Flag has parallel lines, pennant has converging lines
- **Duration**: Should be brief — 5-15 bars. Longer = loses reliability.

### Ascending Triangle (Bullish)
- **Structure**: Flat resistance line on top, rising support line on bottom. Higher lows pushing into flat ceiling.
- **Entry**: Break above flat resistance
- **Target**: Height of triangle projected up from breakout
- **Stop**: Below the last higher low
- **Reliability**: HIGH for bullish breakout (70%+ break upward)
- **Key insight**: Each higher low shows increasing buying pressure against a fixed ceiling. Eventually buyers overwhelm.
- **Volume**: Should decrease during formation, spike on breakout
- **Failure**: Break below the rising support = bearish signal (relatively rare)

### Descending Triangle (Bearish)
- **Structure**: Flat support line on bottom, declining resistance on top. Lower highs pressing into flat floor.
- **Entry**: Break below flat support
- **Target**: Height of triangle projected down
- **Reliability**: HIGH for bearish breakout (70%+ break downward)
- **Mirror of ascending triangle**

### Symmetrical Triangle (Bilateral)
- **Structure**: Converging trendlines — lower highs AND higher lows. Price coiling.
- **Entry**: Break in either direction. Trade the breakout direction.
- **Target**: Widest part of triangle projected from breakout
- **Stop**: Beyond the opposite side of triangle
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM — can break either way, so wait for clear break with volume
- **Bias**: Slight tendency to break in the direction of the prior trend (55-60%)
- **Key rule**: Breakout should occur in the first 75% of the triangle. If price reaches the apex, pattern loses strength.

### Rectangle / Range (Bilateral Continuation)
- **Structure**: Horizontal support and resistance with price bouncing between them
- **Entry**: Break above resistance (bullish) or below support (bearish). Can also trade the range (buy at support, sell at resistance).
- **Target**: Height of rectangle projected from breakout
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM for breakout direction. HIGH for range-bound trading within it.
- **Key insight**: The longer the rectangle, the more powerful the eventual breakout

## ADVANCED PATTERNS

### Rising Wedge (Bearish Reversal / Continuation)
- **Structure**: Two converging upward-sloping trendlines. Both highs and lows are rising, but the range is narrowing.
- **Entry**: Break below the lower trendline
- **Target**: Height of wedge at its widest point, projected down
- **Stop**: Above the last swing high within the wedge
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM-HIGH — strong when it appears after an extended uptrend
- **What it means**: Buying is weakening. Each push higher has less momentum. Sellers are gradually taking over.
- **Key difference from channel**: In a channel, lines are parallel. In a wedge, they converge.

### Falling Wedge (Bullish Reversal / Continuation)
- **Structure**: Two converging downward-sloping trendlines. Both highs and lows falling but range narrowing.
- **Entry**: Break above upper trendline
- **Target**: Widest part of wedge projected up
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM-HIGH — strong when appearing after extended downtrend
- **What it means**: Selling pressure exhausting. Each push lower is weaker.

### Broadening Formation / Megaphone (Reversal)
- **Structure**: Expanding range — higher highs AND lower lows. Opposite of triangle. Lines diverge.
- **Entry**: Difficult to trade within. Best traded on the final breakout or at the pattern boundaries.
- **Target**: No clear measured target. Use other levels (S/R, Fibonacci).
- **Reliability**: LOW-MEDIUM — chaotic price action, typically appears at market tops
- **What it means**: Increasing volatility and uncertainty. Big institutions are battling. Often precedes significant reversal.
- **Warning**: Very difficult to trade. Validator should flag this as "unstable conditions" and lean toward SKIP unless at pattern boundary with other confluence.

### Island Reversal
- **Structure**: Price gaps away from a trend, consolidates, then gaps back creating an "island" of prices separated by gaps on both sides
- **Context**: Very rare in forex (requires gaps). Appears at session boundaries or around major news.
- **Reliability**: HIGH when it occurs — but extremely rare on M15 forex
- **What it means**: Sentiment completely reversed. The island represents trapped traders.
- **Trade**: Trade in the direction of the second gap. Stop beyond the island.

### Cup and Handle (Bullish Continuation)
- **Structure**: U-shaped rounding bottom (cup), followed by small pullback (handle), then breakout above the cup's rim
- **Entry**: Break above the handle's high / cup rim resistance
- **Target**: Depth of the cup projected up from breakout
- **Stop**: Below the handle's low
- **Reliability**: MEDIUM-HIGH — more common on daily charts, but can appear on H1/H4
- **Duration**: Typically too slow for M15 trading. Look for this on H4 charts for swing context.
- **Key rules**:
  - Cup should be U-shaped (not V-shaped — V is too aggressive)
  - Handle should retrace 30-50% of cup's right side (not deeper)
  - Volume should increase on breakout

## PATTERN + THESIS FRAMEWORK INTEGRATION

### Which Patterns Appear at Which Thesis Phase

| Thesis Phase | Common Patterns | Validator Action |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Phase 1 (Cross detected) | No clear pattern yet — just EMA cross | WATCH — too early for patterns |
| Phase 2 (Fan separating) | Bull/bear flags, pennants during pullbacks | WATCH → TRADE if flag breaks with trend |
| Phase 2.5 (E100 retest) | Hammer/engulfing at E100, tweezer bottom at E100 | HIGH value — this is the primary entry signal |
| Phase 3 (Full fan expansion) | Continuation flags/pennants, marubozu | TRADE_NOW if adding, or let existing trade run |
| Phase 3.5 (Fan peaking) | Doji, spinning tops at extremes | WATCH — possible reversal forming |
| Phase 4 (Retracement) | Rising/falling wedge, triangle compression | WATCH for re-entry at E55/E100 |
| Phase 5 (Re-acceleration) | Engulfing at E55, flag break in trend direction | TRADE_NOW — re-entry signal |

### Pattern Failure = Strong Signal
When a pattern fails (breaks the wrong way), the resulting move is often MORE powerful than if the pattern had worked. This is because trapped traders scramble to exit.
- Failed H&S (breaks above head) → aggressive long
- Failed double bottom (breaks below) → aggressive short
- Failed ascending triangle (breaks below rising support) → swift selloff

## VALIDATOR PATTERN CHECKLIST

Before acting on any chart pattern:
1. Is the pattern at a key level (S/R, EMA, BB)?
2. Does the higher timeframe trend support this pattern's direction?
3. Is there RSI/MACD divergence confirming the reversal patterns?
4. Is BB expanding or contracting? (Patterns in BB squeeze = potential explosive break)
5. How many bars has the pattern been forming? (Fresh patterns > exhausted patterns)
6. Is there confluence with the thesis framework phase?
7. What does the fan state say? (Pattern WITH fan direction = high conviction)
