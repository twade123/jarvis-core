## Skill Reference
### Loss Aversion Implementation

People feel losses 2x more intensely than equivalent gains. Frame your offers around what customers lose by not acting.

**Weak:** "Save 20% this month"  
**Strong:** "Don't lose your 20% discount—expires tomorrow"  
**Why:** The second triggers loss aversion by positioning the discount as something already possessed that could be taken away.

**Email subject lines:**
- Weak: "Get free shipping"
- Strong: "Your free shipping expires tonight"

### Social Proof Specificity

Generic social proof fails. Specific, relatable proof converts.

**Weak:** "Join thousands of satisfied customers"  
**Strong:** "2,847 software engineers upgraded their home office this month"  
**Why:** Specific numbers feel real, and similar others influence more than generic crowds.

**Anti-pattern:** Fake counters that reset or obviously false numbers ("999,999 people viewed this today"). Users recognize manipulation and trust plummets.

### Anchoring in Pricing

First number seen becomes reference point for all subsequent judgments. Control the anchor.

**Implementation checklist:**
- Show highest-tier pricing first, not lowest
- Display original price before discount price
- Use competitor pricing as anchor ("Others charge $500, we charge $200")
- Anchor on value metrics, not just price ("Save 40 hours per month")

**Weak:** Basic ($10), Pro ($30), Enterprise ($100)  
**Strong:** Enterprise ($100), Pro ($30), Basic ($10)  
**Why:** High anchor makes middle option feel reasonable.

### Scarcity vs. Urgency

Scarcity = limited quantity. Urgency = limited time. Different psychological triggers.

**Scarcity works when:**
- Inventory is actually limited
- Product has high perceived value
- Audience expects competition for the item

**Urgency works when:**
- Decision complexity is high (deadline forces action)
- Benefits are clear but commitment is difficult
- Audience tends to procrastinate

**Anti-pattern:** Fake countdown timers that reset. Users notice and brand trust is permanently damaged.

### Framing Effect Application

Same information, different presentation, different decisions.

**Gain frame** (when audience is risk-averse):
- "This investment grows your savings"
- Use for: Established products, conservative audiences, long-term commitments

**Loss frame** (when audience is risk-seeking):
- "Without this, you're losing $500 monthly"
- Use for: New categories, disruption messaging, overcoming status quo

**Reference point shift:**
- Weak: "$50/month"
- Strong: "Less than $2 per day"
- Why: Daily framing shifts reference point to coffee/lunch pricing.

### Commitment and Consistency

People align future actions with past commitments, especially public or written ones.

**Implementation tactics:**
- Ask for small initial commitments before large ones
- Make commitments visible to others
- Use progressive profiling to build commitment ladder
- Reference past behavior ("You downloaded our guide because...")

**Email sequence example:**
1. Download free resource (small commitment)
2. Take assessment (medium commitment + data)
3. Join community (public commitment)
4. Upgrade to paid (consistent with identity)

### Paradox of Choice

Too many options decrease satisfaction and increase abandonment.

**Choice architecture checklist:**
- Limit options to 3-5 per category
- Highlight one "recommended" option
- Use progressive disclosure for complex choices
- Group related options to reduce cognitive load

**Weak:** 12 pricing tiers with overlapping features  
**Strong:** 3 clear tiers with one marked "Most Popular"

**Menu psychology:** First and last items get more attention. Place high-margin items in these positions.

### Authority and Expertise Signals

People defer to perceived experts, but authority must feel legitimate.

**Credibility markers:**
- Specific credentials, not generic titles
- Media mentions from recognizable outlets
- Quantified expertise ("10,000 hours," "500+ clients")
- Industry-specific knowledge demonstration

**Weak:** "Marketing expert John Smith"  
**Strong:** "John Smith, former Head of Growth at Shopify, featured in Harvard Business Review"

**Anti-pattern:** Bought badges and fake credentials. Research takes 30 seconds—authenticity is table stakes.

### Reciprocity Sequence

Give value before asking for commitment. Obligation creates action.

**Implementation ladder:**
1. Immediate value (free tool, instant result)
2. Personalized insight (assessment, custom report)
3. Ongoing value (newsletter, community access)
4. Request upgrade (obligation established)

**Weak:** Gate everything behind email signup  
**Strong:** Give immediate value, then capture email for "more like this"

**Value stacking:** Layer multiple gives before first ask. Each gift compounds obligation.

### Peak-End Rule

Experiences are judged by peak moment and final moment, not duration or average.

**Customer journey optimization:**
- Design one standout moment in onboarding
- End interactions on positive notes
- Sequence difficult tasks before peak moments
- Make cancellation process unexpectedly pleasant

**Email optimization:** End newsletters with unexpected value (bonus tip, exclusive offer) rather than standard footer.
